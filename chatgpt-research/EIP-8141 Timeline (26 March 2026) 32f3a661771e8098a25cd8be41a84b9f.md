# EIP-8141 Timeline (26 March 2026)

I pulled together a reconstruction of EIP-8141’s evolution from the current spec, the Magicians thread, and the closed or merged PRs tied to 8141. A few GitHub comment threads partially failed to render in the browser, so this is detailed but still bounded by what GitHub exposed cleanly in-page. ([GitHub](https://github.com/ethereum/EIPs/blob/master/EIPS/eip-8141.md))

## Executive read

EIP-8141 began on January 29, 2026 as “Frame Transaction,” a new typed transaction (`0x06`) meant to make transaction validation and gas payment programmable instead of being hard-wired to a single ECDSA signature. The earliest public rationale was twofold: native account abstraction and a path away from Ethereum’s current ECDSA dependency, especially toward post-quantum-friendly authentication without enshrining one new signature scheme directly into the protocol. ([GitHub](https://github.com/ethereum/EIPs/pull/11202))

Since launch, the spec has moved in five big directions: fixing signature-hash and approval semantics, making `APPROVE` more compatible with existing smart accounts, adding EOA default-code support, adding batching and approval-scope flags inside `mode`, and then formalizing a public mempool policy with recognized validation prefixes, canonical paymaster handling, and revalidation rules. ([GitHub](https://github.com/ethereum/EIPs/pull/11205))

## 1. How the spec originally started

The initial proposal was merged as PR #11202 on January 29, 2026. In its original form, the transaction payload was:

```
[chain_id, nonce, sender, frames, max_priority_fee_per_gas, max_fee_per_gas, max_fee_per_blob_gas, blob_versioned_hashes]
frames = [[mode, target, gas_limit, data], ...]
```

and it defined three execution modes: `DEFAULT`, `VERIFY`, and `SENDER`. It also introduced `APPROVE` plus a `TXPARAMLOAD / TXPARAMSIZE / TXPARAMCOPY` family for introspecting the transaction from EVM code. ([GitHub](https://github.com/ethereum/EIPs/pull/11202))

The original design philosophy was already visible in the Magicians thread the same day. The authors argued that frames were not mainly about “multicall,” but about protocol-visible introspection: the protocol needs to reason about validation and fee-payment behavior in a bounded way so these abstract transactions can still be simulated and propagated over p2p. They also explicitly framed the proposal as a response to Ethereum’s long-term ECDSA lock-in and as a native-AA path that can support future cryptography choices without enshrining a single new scheme. ([Fellowship of Ethereum Magicians](https://ethereum-magicians.org/t/frame-transaction/27617))

In the initial spec, `VERIFY` frame data was supposed to be excluded from the canonical signature hash, `APPROVE` used scope values `0x0/0x1/0x2`, there was no EOA default-code model yet, there was no frame-embedded approval-scope or atomic-batch flag system yet, and the rationale explicitly said “No value in frame.” The early examples also treated batching as something accounts could emulate inside `SENDER` logic rather than something with first-class protocol flags. ([GitHub](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/ethereum/EIPs/master/EIPS/eip-8141.md))

## 2. How the feedback evolved over time

The first wave of feedback was conceptual. On Magicians, people immediately asked about multiple `VERIFY` approvals, atomic multicall semantics, whether multiple call frames were necessary, and whether simpler alternatives like adding signature schemes to existing mechanisms would be enough. Felix Lange clarified that only the first relevant approval should count, and Matt Garnett argued that frames are needed so the protocol can inspect and bound validation behavior rather than just support application-level batching. ([Fellowship of Ethereum Magicians](https://ethereum-magicians.org/t/frame-transaction/27617))

The second wave was about compatibility. PR #11297 argued that requiring `APPROVE` to be executed by the top-level frame made adoption harder for existing smart accounts, especially proxy-based ones, because the outer proxy would not understand the new opcode even if the implementation changed. That pushed the design toward a less rigid approval model. ([GitHub](https://github.com/ethereum/EIPs/pull/11297))

The third wave was about correctness and ergonomics. PR #11344 cleaned up mistaken wording around `APPROVE`, especially the difference between `CALLER` and `ADDRESS`, and it triggered discussion about whether `APPROVE` should be restricted only to `VERIFY` frames. Lightclient argued against such a blanket restriction because stateful frames might still be useful in private or local mempools even if they do not qualify for public propagation. ([GitHub](https://github.com/ethereum/EIPs/pull/11344))

The fourth wave was about practical UX and deployability. PR #11379 added EOA support by specifying “default code” behavior for no-code accounts, so EOAs could use frame transactions too. That was a major broadening of scope: 8141 was no longer just a smart-account-native primitive, but a path for today’s EOA-heavy user base to get gas abstraction and sponsored flows. The rationale section in the current spec now explicitly says that EOAs can benefit from gas abstraction, sponsored transactions, and ERC-20 gas payment. ([GitHub](https://github.com/ethereum/EIPs/pull/11379))

The fifth wave was about public mempool safety. In the Magicians thread, Matt explicitly linked 8141 to earlier failed attempts like EIP-2938, saying the missing piece was protocol-level introspection and a workable p2p txpool ruleset. That concern later materialized in PR #11415, which adds a full mempool policy and leans on a canonical paymaster model to avoid ERC-7562-style staking and reputation complexity in the public mempool. ([Fellowship of Ethereum Magicians](https://ethereum-magicians.org/t/frame-transaction/27617))

## 3. What changes were merged over time, and why

### Jan 29, 2026: initial proposal plus immediate sig-hash fix

PR #11202 introduced the EIP. The same day, PR #11205 fixed a missing condition in the signature hash logic so `VERIFY` frame data was properly elided. That fix matters because signatures can live inside arbitrary frames, and the `VERIFY` payload itself should not be committed the same way as executable transaction content. ([GitHub](https://github.com/ethereum/EIPs/pull/11202))

### Feb 10, 2026: relax top-level `APPROVE` requirement

PR #11297 changed the requirement that `APPROVE` must be called by the top-level frame. The stated reason was easier adoption for existing smart accounts and proxies, whose outer proxy code cannot magically learn a new opcode even if the implementation behind it changes. ([GitHub](https://github.com/ethereum/EIPs/pull/11297))

### Feb 21 to Mar 2, 2026: fix `APPROVE` semantics and wording

PR #11344 corrected several issues: it replaced incorrect wording implying `CALLER == frame.target` with the intended `ADDRESS == frame.target`, clarified that frame reverts do not automatically affect later frames, and initially tried to restrict `APPROVE` to `VERIFY` frames. In review, Lightclient agreed the `ADDRESS` fix was correct but pushed back on the `VERIFY`-only restriction because builders may want stateful approving frames outside the public txpool. That restriction was then removed before merge. ([GitHub](https://github.com/ethereum/EIPs/pull/11344))

### Mar 5 to Mar 10, 2026: add EOA support

PR #11379 introduced default-code behavior for EOAs. Derek Chiang summarized the proposal as: if a `VERIFY` frame targets an account with no code, the frame interprets its data as a native signature payload, verifies it against the frame target and sighash, and then executes `APPROVE(scope)`. The PR also proposed adding a `value` field to frames, although the current spec still keeps the outer frame tuple as `[mode, target, gas_limit, data]` and explicitly keeps “no built-in value field” at the transaction-frame level, instead encoding ETH sends via sender-controlled calls. ([GitHub](https://github.com/ethereum/EIPs/pull/11379))

### Mar 11 to Mar 25, 2026: add atomic batching

PR #11395 started by proposing a `SENDER_ATOMIC` mode. The reasoning was that some workflows, such as `approve + swap`, need grouped rollback semantics, and that allowing gas approval inside such atomic sender frames would complicate validation, so approval inside those sender-style atomic frames was explicitly ruled out. In review, commenters, including you, argued atomicity should perhaps be the default, while others argued non-atomic behavior is more general and useful for conditional flows or paymaster interactions. The final spec no longer contains `SENDER_ATOMIC`; instead it uses an atomic-batch flag in `mode`, with bit 11 valid only for `SENDER` frames. That shows the concept was merged, but the representation evolved from a new mode into a flag-based encoding. ([GitHub](https://github.com/ethereum/EIPs/pull/11395))

### Mar 12 to Mar 13, 2026: refactor introspection opcodes and add approval bits

PR #11400 changed the opcode design: instead of a broad `TXPARAMLOAD / TXPARAMSIZE / TXPARAMCOPY` family, the spec now has `TXPARAM`, `FRAMEDATALOAD`, and `FRAMEDATACOPY`. Felix’s rationale was that most transaction parameters are 32 bytes or less, so generic copy/size opcodes were overkill; only frame input data is truly variable-length and needs dedicated accessors. ([GitHub](https://github.com/ethereum/EIPs/pull/11400))

PR #11401 then added approval bits into the upper part of `mode`. Felix’s explanation was that this lets the user constrain what a frame may approve while still signing directly over the canonical sighash. It also created room for additional future mode flags and brought security benefits by narrowing approval authority. PR #11402 immediately adjusted the documented bit numbering to bits 9 and 10, using conventional one-indexed bit terminology. ([GitHub](https://github.com/ethereum/EIPs/pull/11401))

### Mar 13, 2026: an alternative approval-bit simplification was discussed but not merged

PR #11404 proposed simplifying approval bits so accounts could pass the result of `TXPARAM` straight into `APPROVE` without manually manipulating bits. The PR description says this came from discussion with Felix, but the PR was later closed. That means the project explicitly considered a more ergonomic approval-bit interpretation but chose not to land it, at least in that form. ([GitHub](https://github.com/ethereum/EIPs/pull/11404))

### Mar 16 to Mar 25, 2026: formal public mempool policy

PR #11415 added an initial mempool policy. Lightclient’s stated motivation was to avoid the complexity of ERC-7562-style staking and reputation by relying on a canonical paymaster contract model. The current spec now includes validation prefixes, recognized public-mempool transaction shapes, canonical and non-canonical paymaster handling, balance reservation rules, and revalidation logic after new blocks. ([GitHub](https://github.com/ethereum/EIPs/pull/11415))

### Mar 21, 2026: attempted migration to EIP-7932 registry was rejected

PR #11408 proposed migrating EOA default-code behavior to the EIP-7932 registry. Lightclient closed it, saying the authors want to preserve the ability to define custom behavior in 8141’s default contract and do not want to rely on another EIP or precompile for that behavior. So the current direction keeps EOA default-code semantics self-contained inside 8141. ([GitHub](https://github.com/ethereum/EIPs/pull/11408))

### Mar 26, 2026: default code updated to match latest spec

PR #11448 updated the default-code logic after approval bits had landed. Derek Chiang’s summary was simple: with approval bits now supported, default code could be simplified. The current default-code section reflects that by deriving approval scope from `mode` bits rather than from a more bespoke per-signature payload scheme. ([GitHub](https://github.com/ethereum/EIPs/pull/11448))

## 4. What is distinct in the latest spec versus the original spec

Here are the biggest deltas between the original January 29 draft and the latest March 26 draft.

First, the introspection opcode model changed. Originally the EIP defined `TXPARAMLOAD`, `TXPARAMSIZE`, and `TXPARAMCOPY`; today it defines `TXPARAM`, `FRAMEDATALOAD`, and `FRAMEDATACOPY`. This is a meaningful redesign, not just renaming: the current spec narrows generic transaction introspection and gives variable-length access only where it is actually needed. ([GitHub](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/ethereum/EIPs/master/EIPS/eip-8141.md))

Second, `mode` is now much richer. Originally `mode` only selected among `DEFAULT`, `VERIFY`, and `SENDER`. In the latest spec, the lower bits still encode the execution mode, but the upper bits now carry approval scope and atomic-batch flags. Bits 9-10 encode approval scope, and bit 11 is an atomic-batch flag valid only for `SENDER` frames. ([GitHub](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/ethereum/EIPs/master/EIPS/eip-8141.md))

Third, approval semantics changed materially. In the original text, `APPROVE` scopes were `0x0`, `0x1`, and `0x2`; in the latest spec they are `0x1`, `0x2`, and `0x3`, with the allowed scope constrained by frame mode bits. The spec also now spells out how approval-scope bits gate which `APPROVE` scopes are legal in a frame. ([GitHub](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/ethereum/EIPs/master/EIPS/eip-8141.md))

Fourth, the sig-hash definition became more precise. The current draft explicitly elides data for any frame whose low mode bits equal `VERIFY`, not just literal equality on the whole `mode` word. That is important now that `mode` can also carry flags. ([GitHub](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/ethereum/EIPs/master/EIPS/eip-8141.md))

Fifth, EOA support is a major new capability. The original rationale said there was no built-in value field and assumed account code would perform value transfers. The latest spec still keeps no built-in frame-level value field, but it now adds “default code” for no-code accounts, with explicit secp256k1 and P-256 verification behavior, which lets EOAs use frame transactions for gas abstraction and sponsored flows. ([GitHub](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/ethereum/EIPs/master/EIPS/eip-8141.md))

Sixth, the latest draft includes a full public mempool policy, which the original spec did not. That new section defines the “validation prefix,” recognized public-mempool frame layouts, structural rules, trace rules, canonical paymaster exceptions, paymaster balance reservation, and revalidation on chain-head changes. ([GitHub](https://github.com/ethereum/EIPs/blob/master/EIPS/eip-8141.md))

## 5. What EIP-8141 can do today and how it works

Today’s 8141 defines a new typed transaction, `FRAME_TX_TYPE = 0x06`, where the sender submits a list of frames. Each frame has a `mode`, `target`, `gas_limit`, and `data`. The transaction executes frame-by-frame, with the protocol tracking whether execution has been approved on behalf of the sender and whether someone has approved paying gas. `VERIFY` frames run as `ENTRY_POINT`, must call `APPROVE`, and have their `data` elided from the canonical sighash. `SENDER` frames run as if sent by `tx.sender`, but only after sender approval. ([GitHub](https://github.com/ethereum/EIPs/blob/master/EIPS/eip-8141.md))

The core primitive is that validation and fee payment become programmable. A contract can inspect canonical transaction parameters, verify signatures using arbitrary logic, decide whether to authorize execution, and separately decide whether to authorize paying gas. In the current default-code model, EOAs with no runtime code are still usable because the protocol specifies fallback behavior for verifying secp256k1 and P-256 signatures and then calling `APPROVE(scope)` accordingly. ([GitHub](https://github.com/ethereum/EIPs/blob/master/EIPS/eip-8141.md))

The latest spec supports several practical patterns. It supports a simple self-relayed transaction where the sender verifies itself and approves both execution and payment. It supports account deployment before verification. It supports atomic grouped sender operations via the atomic-batch flag, for cases like `approve + swap`. It supports sponsored transactions where a separate paymaster verifies and approves gas payment, including ERC-20-based gas flows. And because of default-code behavior, it supports EOA users participating in those same gas-abstraction flows. ([GitHub](https://github.com/ethereum/EIPs/blob/master/EIPS/eip-8141.md))

For public-mempool use, the spec now sharply narrows what nodes are expected to accept. Only transactions whose validation prefix matches one of four recognized patterns are eligible for public propagation, and public mempool validation only cares about frames up to the point where `payer_approved = true`. This is the protocol’s answer to “native AA without losing txpool safety”: keep the transaction generic overall, but constrain the validation prefix enough that nodes can simulate it safely. ([GitHub](https://github.com/ethereum/EIPs/blob/master/EIPS/eip-8141.md))

The latest public-mempool model also adds a canonical-paymaster path. Canonical paymaster instances are identified by exact runtime-code match, and nodes reserve the transaction’s maximum cost against the paymaster’s available balance. Non-canonical paymasters are still allowed, but under tighter limits, including a cap on how many pending transactions can rely on the same non-canonical paymaster. ([GitHub](https://github.com/ethereum/EIPs/pull/11415))

## My synthesis of the spec’s trajectory

The through-line is pretty clear: 8141 started as a general native-AA and post-ECDSA transaction format, then spent the next two months being hardened into something clients and wallet authors could plausibly reason about. The early drafts were mostly about expressive power; the later drafts are about operational constraints: compatibility with existing accounts, safe approval semantics, EOA migration, explicit batching semantics, and especially txpool admissibility. ([Fellowship of Ethereum Magicians](https://ethereum-magicians.org/t/frame-transaction/27617))

A second clear pattern is that the authors repeatedly preferred “generic primitive plus protocol-visible structure” over “hard-coded use case.” You can see that in the Magicians responses about why frames exist, in the move from `SENDER_ATOMIC` to a mode-flag approach, and in the decision not to outsource default-code behavior to EIP-7932 yet. The spec keeps trying to stay flexible while still making the validation prefix structured enough for the network to reason about. ([Fellowship of Ethereum Magicians](https://ethereum-magicians.org/t/frame-transaction/27617))

A third pattern is that 8141 is increasingly two specs at once: an execution model and a mempool model. The execution model says “validation and payment are programmable”; the mempool model says “that programmability is only publicly relayable when it fits a small set of validation-prefix shapes and state-dependency rules.” That split feels central to understanding where the EIP stands today. ([GitHub](https://github.com/ethereum/EIPs/blob/master/EIPS/eip-8141.md))
